Understanding Torque
When a ballerina prepares to execute a pirouette, they first begin with a set position which is one foot forward, one foot back, legs slightly bent and the body balanced in the middle. Then the dancer will push off their back foot which will create momentum and give them enough speed to turn around themselves. Based on experience, I would say this prep is extremely effective due to the physics concept of torque. Essentially, Torque is the force that causes an object to rotate around an axis which in this case is the dancer's body.


Equation for Torque = Force X Lever Arm (Distance from the axis)


Connection to Dance:

How does a ballerina generate torque one might wonder? Well, in dance the ballerina generates torque by pushing off the ground with their foot. The harder they push, the greater the force applied. This initial push is important as it provides the energy needed to start the rotation. The body then rotates around an imaginary vertical line running through the center of the body, usually through the spine. This is the axis of rotation or better known as your center. The distance from the point of the foot’s push-off (where the force is applied) to the axis (center of the body) affects the amount of torque generated. By keeping their body compact—bringing the arms and legs close to the body—the dancer reduces the radius, which affects rotational inertia and is also why they often bring their arms closer to their body. This allows them to spin faster and for a longer time. The more torque the dancer can produce, the more force they have going into their pirouette, allowing them to turn multiple times before friction slows them down.
Importance of staying centered
Staying centered is crucial because any shift in your axis of rotation reduces balance and control. If the center of mass moves off the rotational axis, you may start wobbling or even fall out of the turn. This is because an off-center position disrupts the conservation of angular momentum, requiring more energy to stabilize.

Mass distribution refers to how a dancer's mass is spread around their axis of rotation (typically through the center of the body). To perform multiple pirouettes or faster spins, they pull their arms close to the body which reduces the distance between mass and the axis of rotation, leading to faster turns. Lowering or raising the arms or adjusting the leg position can affect balance and speed. For example, raising arms too high could shift the center of mass and destabilize the turn, while lowering them to waist level can improve balance.


How to stay centered?

Staying centered is very important when doing pirouettes and a way that is used often to do this is by spotting. Spotting is crucial for maintaining balance and avoiding dizziness. It involves focusing on a fixed point with the eyes and moving the head around quickly to return to that point after each turn. This helps the dancer maintain their orientation and enhances the control of the turn by creating stability in the upper body. By fixing on a spot, you tend to minimize the chance of losing balance/falling out of the pirouette. In other ways, spotting also allows the body to rotate faster while the head "delays" the turn slightly, creating the appearance of more controlled and sharp rotations.


Improvement Tip: Try to focus on “spotting” (fixing your gaze on a single point) to keep your center of mass aligned. Also, strengthening your core, as a stable core helps in maintaining the center of mass along the rotation axis, allowing you to complete controlled turns without leaning or tilting.
